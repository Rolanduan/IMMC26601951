\section{Technical Derivations}
This appendix collects the mathematical pieces behind the new framework so that the manuscript stays readable while the technical closure remains explicit.

\subsection{Uniqueness of the Liability-Field Estimator}
\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:field}
If \(\omega_i>0\) for every cell and \(\rho\ge 0\), then the objective in Equation~\eqref{eq:prior},
\[
f(\ell)=\sum_{i=1}^{64}\omega_i(\ell_i-\widetilde \ell_i)^2+\rho\,\ell^\top L_G\ell,
\]
is strictly convex and therefore has a unique minimizer.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The first term is a weighted quadratic with Hessian \(2\operatorname{diag}(\omega_1,\dots,\omega_{64})\), which is positive definite because every \(\omega_i>0\). The second term has Hessian \(2\rho L_G\), which is positive semidefinite because any graph Laplacian is positive semidefinite and \(\rho\ge 0\). The sum of a positive definite matrix and a positive semidefinite matrix is positive definite, so \(f(\ell)\) is strictly convex. A strictly convex coercive quadratic has a unique minimizer.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:field} is the formal reason the liability surface is well defined. The fitted field does not depend on arbitrary tie-breaking or heuristic smoothing.

\subsection{Monotonicity and Diminishing Returns of Protection Quality}
\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:quality}
Let
\[
p_i(a)=1-\exp(-q_i),
\qquad
q_i=\alpha F_i+\beta y_i+\gamma\sum_j b_{ij}z_j.
\]
Then \(p_i(a)\) is increasing and concave in each decision component that enters \(q_i\).
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Treat \(p(q)=1-e^{-q}\) on \(q\ge 0\). Then \(p'(q)=e^{-q}>0\) and \(p''(q)=-e^{-q}<0\). Hence \(p(q)\) is increasing and concave in \(q\). Because \(q_i\) is affine in \(F_i\), \(y_i\), and \(z_j\), composition preserves monotonicity and concavity in each component. Therefore additional assets always improve protection quality but with diminishing returns.
\end{proof}

This proposition justifies the portfolio logic in Equation~\eqref{eq:dose}: spreading a small amount of effort to a new expensive cell can be more valuable than oversaturating a cell that is already heavily protected.

\subsection{CVaR Epigraph Reformulation}
\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:cvar}
For any finite scenario set \(\Omega\), the quantity
\[
\eta + \frac{1}{(1-\alpha)|\Omega|}\sum_{\omega\in\Omega}\zeta_\omega
\]
subject to \(\zeta_\omega\ge Y_\omega(a)-\eta\) and \(\zeta_\omega\ge 0\) is an exact epigraph formulation of \(\operatorname{CVaR}_\alpha(Y(a))\).
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
This is the standard Rockafellar--Uryasev representation of conditional value at risk for a discrete scenario distribution. For any fixed decision \(a\), minimizing the epigraph expression over \(\eta\) and \(\zeta_\omega\) yields the average of the worst \((1-\alpha)\)-tail of the loss distribution. Therefore the objective in Equation~\eqref{eq:obj} exactly incorporates \(\operatorname{CVaR}_\alpha\) rather than a heuristic tail penalty.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:cvar} is what makes the new model genuinely different from a weighted-average patrol objective: bad months are penalized explicitly.

\subsection{Workload Guarantee Under Sub-Gaussian Tails}
\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:workload}
If the station workload \(A_{s,t}\) is sub-Gaussian with mean \(\mu_s\) and scale parameter \(\sigma_s\), then choosing
\[
c_s
\ge
\mu_s-b^\star+\sqrt{2\sigma_s^2\log(1/\epsilon_s)}
\]
guarantees
\[
\Pr(A_{s,t}>c_s+b^\star)\le \epsilon_s.
\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
By the assumed sub-Gaussian tail bound,
\[
\Pr(A_{s,t}-\mu_s>x)\le \exp\!\left(-\frac{x^2}{2\sigma_s^2}\right).
\]
Set \(x=c_s+b^\star-\mu_s\). If
\[
x\ge \sqrt{2\sigma_s^2\log(1/\epsilon_s)},
\]
then
\[
\Pr(A_{s,t}>c_s+b^\star)
\le
\exp\!\left(-\frac{x^2}{2\sigma_s^2}\right)
\le
\epsilon_s.
\]
This proves the claim.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:workload} justifies the staffing rule in Equation~\eqref{eq:rangermin}: the ranger floor follows from a probabilistic service covenant, not from a descriptive average.

\section{Algorithmic Workflow}
The following four procedures summarize the paper's end-to-end logic in executable form.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Algorithm A1: Build the ecological-liability surface.}
\label{alg:app_transport}
\begin{tabular}{p{0.95\linewidth}}
\toprule
\textbf{Input:} official basemap, ecology layers, road graph, station locations, access variables, monitoring recency. \\
\textbf{Output:} latent intensity \(\lambda_i\), information deficit \(U_i\), service drag \(D_i\), liability field \(L_i\). \\

\section{Managerial Reading}
\textbf{Liability field}: where ecological failure is most expensive once uncertainty and service drag are priced together.\\
\textbf{Contract cells}: the small subset of cells that must not fall below a minimum protection standard.\\
\textbf{Portfolio design}: how patrol templates, sentinels, and reserve reduce average liability and tail risk together.\\
\textbf{Workload guarantee}: how many teams are required so that stations do not fail on bad days.\\

Together, these modules describe Etosha not as an area to be painted, but as a liability contract to be honored.

\section*{AI Use Report}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{AI Use Report}
This project uses AI as a drafting and coding copilot, not as an autonomous scientific authority. AI assistance was used to accelerate manuscript structuring, symbolic formatting, figure scripting, and language revision. All equations, assumptions, parameter interpretations, and management conclusions were then reviewed for internal consistency against the intended operating logic of the paper.

The benefit of AI in this workflow is speed across text, code, and figure iteration. The main risk is synthetic fluency without technical closure. For that reason, we used explicit safeguards: equation-to-claim checks, contract-feasibility checks, overload-guarantee checks, and repeated review of whether the proposed field logic really matches the management interpretation.

Responsibility for the final scientific content remains with the authors.
