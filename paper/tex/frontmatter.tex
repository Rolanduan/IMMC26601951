\section*{Summary Sheet}
We reformulate wildlife protection in Etosha National Park as an \emph{ecological-liability contract} rather than a patrol-coverage or corridor-blocking problem. The central decision question is no longer ``Which route should be sealed first?'' It is ``Which zones carry the largest expected ecological liability once incident intensity, information uncertainty, and field service drag are all priced into the same risk measure?'' To answer that question, we build a new three-layer stack: a GIS-grounded liability field estimated by graph-regularized intensity fitting, an ambiguity-aware protection portfolio that minimizes mean loss plus tail risk, and a workload-guarantee layer that sizes ranger teams from overload probabilities instead of average hours.

That restatement produces a visibly different management answer. Etosha is best managed as a \emph{liability ridge with a protection contract}: top-decile cells receive guaranteed patrol recurrence or sentinel presence, surrounding cells are handled through a risk-premium portfolio, and UAV capacity is held as flexible recourse for the stations whose workload volatility most threatens service reliability. Under the same baseline resources, the resulting design raises the protected-liability ratio from 0.65 to 0.88, increases mean event confirmation from 0.46 to 0.67, lowers station-overload probability from 0.34 to 0.16, and lifts the protection index from 0.57 to 0.76 while preserving a staffing floor near 34 field rangers.

The contribution of this paper is therefore not another patrol map. It is a new decision object. We optimize expected ecological liability, uncertainty shrinkage, and tail-risk control in one contract-based model. That changes the practical conclusion as well: Etosha should not spread effort thinly across a long visible corridor, and it should not overcommit UAVs to static sectors. Instead, managers should guarantee minimum protection quality on the highest-liability cells, stabilize sentinel uptime, and hold mobile reserve for the stations whose workload risk most threatens the service covenant.

{\bf Keywords:} ecological liability surface; graph-regularized intensity field; CVaR protection portfolio; workload guarantee; protected-area analytics.

\section*{Letter to IMMC}
Dear International Mission for Monitoring Conservation (IMMC),

This paper begins from a stricter operational premise. Large reserves do not fail merely because patrols are sparse. They fail when a small set of ecologically expensive zones remains simultaneously attractive to intruders, weakly observed, and hard to serve on time. That is a liability problem, not a mileage problem.

We therefore rebuild the analytic architecture from first principles. First, we estimate a park-wide ecological-liability surface by combining biological consequence, latent incident intensity, observability deficit, and service drag on a GIS-aligned grid. Second, we generate a protection portfolio of patrol templates, sentinel devices, and UAV reserve windows by minimizing both expected unresolved liability and the tail risk of bad operating scenarios. Third, we size field staffing by requiring each station to remain below an overload probability target rather than by merely balancing average patrol hours. The resulting system is designed to honor a protection contract on the park's most expensive cells.

This reformulation changes the strategy. The recommended design is not blanket corridor saturation. It is a layered liability contract. Top-decile liability cells receive guaranteed recurrence through patrol templates or persistent sensors; adjacent ridge cells receive portfolio protection according to their marginal risk contribution; and UAV support remains mobile so it can relieve whichever station currently faces the greatest overload risk. Under baseline assumptions, this structure outperforms uniform deployment while sustaining a field-force threshold of roughly 34 rangers.

The policy implications are direct. Managers should protect crew depth before buying additional hardware, because overload risk degrades the value of all other assets. They should protect sentinel uptime before expanding sensor count, because unreliable watch on the most expensive cells creates blind spots that no map-wide average can reveal. They should also evaluate anti-poaching performance with liability reduction, tail-risk control, and overload probability rather than with patrol kilometers alone.

We also recommend a different transfer rule. Parks should not copy a fixed Etosha patrol pattern. They should rebuild the liability surface, uncertainty field, and station workload model for their own geography. What transfers is the contract logic: estimate ecological liability, guarantee minimum protection on the most expensive cells, and staff the field system so that service remains reliable even under bad days.

For the IMMC, that is the central recommendation. Encourage conservation strategies that allocate effort by ecological liability and operational uncertainty, not by map coverage alone.
