\section*{Summary Sheet}
We recast wildlife protection in Etosha National Park as a protected-area operating system rather than a simple patrol-allocation exercise. The design objective is not full spatial coverage; it is reliable protective service in decisive zones where biological value, intrusion opportunity, and delayed response coincide. To do this, we build a four-layer decision stack: a GIS digital twin that converts park geography into operational cells, a priority engine that estimates risk and service deficit, a mixed-integer controller that assigns patrol loops, fixed sensors, and drone sorties, and a stochastic stress test that evaluates whether the resulting deployment remains effective under pressure, delay, and uncertainty.

The resulting strategy is materially different from uniform coverage. In the baseline Etosha scenario, the optimized plan concentrates effort along the eastern--southern corridor, where water dependence, road access, and slow response overlap. Relative to uniform deployment, weighted protection coverage rises from 0.64 to 0.87, mean detection probability rises from 0.47 to 0.66, and the overall protection index improves from 0.56 to 0.74. The system remains schedulable with roughly 34 field rangers (17 two-person teams), 12 fixed devices, and 4 daily drone sorties. Sensitivity results show a clear hierarchy of control levers: staffing is the dominant driver of performance, detection quality is second, patrol cadence is third, and additional devices provide diminishing returns once persistent hotspots are instrumented.

The contribution of this paper is therefore system-level rather than cosmetic. We provide a park-scale operating doctrine, a mathematically explicit controller, a closed-loop update mechanism, a staffing-floor rule, and a transfer protocol for parks with different ecologies and mobility structures. The same logic can be recalibrated for Yellowstone, Kakadu, and similar large reserves without redesigning the model core. In short, we propose not just where to patrol, but how to run wildlife protection as a repeatable decision system.

{\bf Keywords:} wildlife protection operating system; spatial digital twin; anti-poaching control; mixed-integer optimization; stochastic simulation.

\section*{Letter to IMMC}
Dear International Mission for Monitoring Conservation (IMMC),

This paper starts from one practical claim: large protected areas fail when managers ask the wrong operational question. The wrong question is ``How do we cover the whole park?'' The right question is ``How do we keep decisive places reliably protected with limited teams, limited sensors, and imperfect information?'' For Etosha, that distinction changes everything. The park is too large and too heterogeneous for uniform patrol logic to work. Wildlife clusters around waterholes, intrusion opportunities concentrate near roads and gates, and response times vary sharply across the landscape. Protection is therefore a service-reliability problem, not a blanket-coverage problem.

Our solution treats Etosha as a protected-area operating system. First, we build a digital twin of the park that combines ecological pull, access opportunity, and response friction into a risk field. Second, we convert that field into operational priorities by identifying zones where high-value wildlife and exploitable access overlap. Third, we allocate patrol loops, fixed devices, and drone sorties through a constrained optimization model that respects staffing, time, and station-capacity limits. Fourth, we test the resulting plan in a closed-loop simulation where illegal entries, detection, dispatch, and interception evolve over time and feed back into the next round of planning.

This system produces a clear management answer for Etosha. The eastern--southern corridor is the dominant operating theater. Almost half of patrol time should be concentrated there, about one-third should support the southern--central corridor, and the balance should cover lower-risk western and northern sectors. Fixed devices should anchor persistent hotspots, while drones should be reserved for uncertainty-heavy sectors and surge observation. Under baseline assumptions, the system raises the protection index from 0.56 to 0.74 without requiring new resources. It also identifies a hard staffing floor: approximately 34 field rangers, or 17 two-person teams, are needed to keep protection above the recommended operating threshold.

The most important policy implication is that staffing must be protected before technology is expanded. When ranger availability collapses, the system degrades nonlinearly because hotspot revisit discipline breaks. Detection quality is the next most important lever; improving intelligence flow, placement discipline, and device uptime can offset part of a staffing shortfall, but not all of it. The safe management doctrine is therefore straightforward: hold the staffing floor, lock the hotspot layer, use sensors for persistence, use drones for uncertainty reduction, and refresh the plan on a rolling cycle as conditions shift.

We also emphasize accountability. A park-scale protection strategy should not be judged by kilometers patrolled alone. Managers should track repeated hotspot coverage, response-time reliability in high-risk sectors, and monitoring uptime during active patrol windows. Those measures create a direct line from strategy to field performance and make it possible to detect when a plan is drifting from operational reality.

Finally, this framework is transferable because its logic is modular. The map changes from park to park; the operating discipline does not. Any reserve facing sparse resources and spatially uneven risk can use the same sequence: diagnose, prioritize, allocate, stress test, update. For the IMMC, that is the central recommendation. Encourage conservation planning that is selective rather than uniform, operational rather than aspirational, and adaptive rather than static. That is the most defensible path to protecting wildlife at scale.
