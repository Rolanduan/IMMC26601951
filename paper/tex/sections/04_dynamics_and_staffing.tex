\section{Closed-Loop Simulation and Staffing Threshold}
\subsection{Why the Controller Must Be Stress Tested}
An optimization model can produce an elegant static deployment that still fails in practice once incidents arrive irregularly, response is delayed, or detection degrades over time. For that reason, the controller is embedded inside a stochastic stress test. The purpose of Model III is not decorative simulation; it is to answer whether the recommended plan can maintain acceptable service under operational noise and adversarial uncertainty.

\subsection{Event Generation, Detection, and Interception}
Let \(\mathcal{G}_C=(V_C,E_C)\) be the zone graph inherited from the GIS partition. A latent intruder state moves on \(\mathcal{G}_C\) with time-varying transition matrix \(P^t\), where \(d_{ij}\) is the one-step movement cost between adjacent zones \(i\) and \(j\), and for adjacent zones \(i\to j\),
\begin{equation}
P_{ij}^t=
\frac{\exp(\nu_1A_j+\nu_2B_j-\nu_3C_j^t-\nu_4 d_{ij})}
{\sum_{k:(i,k)\in E_C}\exp(\nu_1A_k+\nu_2B_k-\nu_3C_k^t-\nu_4 d_{ik})}.
\label{eq:markov}
\end{equation}
If \(m^t\) is the occupancy distribution over zones, then
\begin{equation}
m^{t+1}=m^tP^t,
\qquad
\lambda_i^t=\lambda_0 m_i^t.
\label{eq:occupancy}
\end{equation}
For competition-scale experiments, we compress this mean-field graph walk into a reduced-form arrival model in which the stationary attraction proxy is the risk field \(R_i\). For each zone \(i\) and day \(t\), illegal activity arrivals follow
\begin{equation}
N_i^t \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_0R_i\Psi_t),
\end{equation}
where \(\Psi_t\) captures temporal pressure such as seasonal concentration, intelligence alerts, or short-run tactical shifts. This means that high-risk cells do not simply receive more attention; they are also exposed to more threat arrivals.

If the active asset set in zone \(i\) on day \(t\) is \(\mathcal{A}_i^t\), then the probability of detection is
\begin{equation}
q_i^t = 1-\prod_{a\in \mathcal{A}_i^t}(1-q_a).
\label{eq:detect}
\end{equation}
An exact capture model on \(\mathcal{G}_C\) is an absorbing Markov chain with transient matrix \(Q^t\) and capture-transition vector \(r_c^t\). If an event starts in zone \(i\), the exact capture probability is
\begin{equation}
\alpha_i^t=\mathbf{e}_i^\top (I-Q^t)^{-1} r_c^t.
\label{eq:absorb}
\end{equation}
The simulation uses the reduced-form surrogate that matters operationally most directly: interception success as a function of realized response delay.
\begin{equation}
g_i^t = \frac{1}{1+\exp[\kappa(\tau_i^t-\tau^\star)]},
\label{eq:intercept}
\end{equation}
where \(\tau_i^t\) is realized response time and \(\tau^\star\) is the critical delay threshold beyond which the probability of neutralization collapses.

The full neutralization probability becomes
\begin{equation}
u_i^t = q_i^t g_i^t.
\end{equation}
The park-wide protection index is
\begin{equation}
\PI = 1-\frac{\sum_{t=1}^T \sum_i R_i L_i^t}{\sum_{t=1}^T \sum_i R_i N_i^t + \varepsilon},
\label{eq:pi}
\end{equation}
where \(L_i^t\) is weighted successful loss. Equation~\eqref{eq:pi} is the core accountability measure: it penalizes failure most strongly in the zones where failure is most consequential.

\subsection{Closed-Form Insight and Response-Time Structure}
Let \(\lambda_i^t = \lambda_0 R_i \Psi_t\). Because arrivals are Poisson and each event is neutralized with probability \(u_i^t\), expected loss satisfies
\[
\mathbb{E}[L_i^t] = \lambda_i^t(1-u_i^t).
\]
Hence
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[\PI] \approx 1-\frac{\sum_{t=1}^T \sum_i R_i \lambda_i^t (1-u_i^t)}{\sum_{t=1}^T \sum_i R_i \lambda_i^t}.
\label{eq:piexp}
\end{equation}
This expression makes the system logic transparent: good control is equivalent to shrinking the weighted average of unneutralized risk.

Response time itself is modeled as the sum of a graph shortest-path base, a dispatch delay, and terrain or communication noise:
\begin{equation}
\tau_i^t = d_i + \delta_t + \xi_i^t,
\label{eq:taudist}
\end{equation}
where \(d_i\) is the multi-source shortest-path term from Equation~\eqref{eq:shortest}, \(\delta_t \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_\delta,\sigma_\delta^2)\) is dispatch or queueing delay, and \(\xi_i^t \sim \text{LogNormal}(\mu_\xi,\sigma_\xi^2)\) is terrain or communication noise. This produces the right-skewed delay pattern expected in field operations, where most responses are routine but a nontrivial minority are slowed by friction.

\subsection{Feedback Update}
The simulation is not terminal; it feeds the next planning cycle. We update patrol pressure and latent intrusion risk by
\begin{align}
C_i^{t+1}&=(1-\omega)C_i^t+\omega\left(\frac{F_i^t}{\max_k F_k^t}\right), \nonumber\\
(I+\lambda_L L)\Omega^{t+1}&=\theta_0\mathbf{1}+\theta_1A+\theta_2B-\theta_3C^{t+1}, \nonumber\\
P_i^{t+1}&=\sigma(\Omega_i^{t+1}),
\label{eq:priorupdate}
\end{align}
where \(\sigma(\cdot)\) is the logistic function and \(L\) is the same zone-graph Laplacian used in Equation~\eqref{eq:prior}. This closes the operating loop: deployment changes local pressure, local pressure changes future risk, and future risk reshapes deployment through the same graph that carries mobility and exposure.

The model is directionally coherent. More patrols, more devices, or more UAV coverage weakly increase surveillance intensity and therefore weakly increase neutralization probability. That monotonicity prevents pathological feedback behavior and makes the control system interpretable.

\subsection{Dispatch Discipline and Daily Control Cycle}
The park is modeled with staggered patrol departures, continuous device monitoring, and two daily drone windows. That timing matters because identical total effort can perform very differently depending on whether coverage is synchronized or spread across the day. In management terms, the system is designed to avoid silent intervals in high-risk zones.

The online scheduling layer lives on the time-expanded road graph \(\mathcal{G}_T=(V_R\times\{0,\dots,H\},E_T)\), where \(H\) is the number of daily time slots. A feasible daily assignment is a set of station-anchored paths on \(\mathcal{G}_T\), and the reduced cost of activating route \(r\) at slot \(\tau\) is
\begin{equation}
\Delta_{r,\tau}^t=
\sum_{i=1}^{64}R_i\!\left[e^{-\eta_i^t}-e^{-(\eta_i^t+\alpha a_{ir})}\right]
-\lambda h_r.
\label{eq:dispatchscore}
\end{equation}
where \(\eta_i^t=\alpha F_i^t+\beta y_i^t+\gamma\sum_j b_{ij}z_j^t\). The exact online problem is a min-cost flow on \(\mathcal{G}_T\). The implementation uses the hotspot-first greedy rule below, which selects the highest positive reduced-cost actions while enforcing station-time feasibility.

\subsubsection{Interpretation}
Two system insights emerge. First, repeated service in decisive cells dominates shallow coverage everywhere. The top-risk quartile contains a majority share of biological consequence despite covering only a quarter of the park area, so the right strategy is saturation of that quartile rather than equitable dispersion. Second, mixed assets outperform patrol-only control because persistent devices raise early warning while drones absorb uncertainty and reduce response ambiguity.

Accordingly, the Etosha operating doctrine has three components:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Hotspot assurance}: lock the top-risk quartile into the recurring service plan.
  \item \textbf{Layered surveillance}: use fixed devices for persistence and drones for adaptive surge support.
  \item \textbf{Staffing protection}: preserve the ranger floor before expanding low-marginal-value technology.
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:simulation} shows the time path of protection under repeated stochastic realizations. The optimized system crosses the command target of \(\PI=0.75\) within roughly one week and remains near that level thereafter, while the uniform strategy never achieves stable threshold crossing. Figure~\ref{fig:robustness} confirms that the optimized controller not only improves the mean outcome but also thins the low-performance tail.

\subsubsection{Feasibility and Minimum Staffing}
The recommended plan must be schedulable, not merely optimal on paper. Under baseline staffing:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Shift feasibility}: 34 field rangers provide \(272\) person-hours per day; with utilization \(\rho=0.78\), usable effort is about \(212\) person-hours.
  \item \textbf{Route feasibility}: the selected package consumes about \(198\) person-hours in expectation, leaving a narrow but meaningful reserve for incidents, delays, and weather shocks.
\end{itemize}

To sustain \(\PI \ge 0.70\), the empirical staffing floor is
\begin{equation}
n_{\min} = \left\lceil \frac{\sum_{i\in \mathcal{H}} f_i^\star \bar{\tau}_i}{h_{\text{shift}}\rho} \right\rceil,
\label{eq:rangermin}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{H}\) is the hotspot set and \(\bar{\tau}_i\) is representative route effort for hotspot \(i\). In the baseline scenario, Equation~\eqref{eq:rangermin} agrees with the stochastic results at approximately 34 field rangers. That is the practical threshold between stable service and fragile coverage.
