\section{Protection Over Time and Staffing Need}
\subsection{Dynamic Protection Simulation Structure}
The allocation model produces a static plan; managers also need to know whether it remains effective over time. We therefore simulate daily operations over \(T=30\) days with 1000 Monte Carlo replications. For each zone \(i\) and day \(t\), illegal activity arrivals follow
\begin{equation}
N_i^t \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_0R_i\Psi_t),
\end{equation}
where \(\Psi_t\) captures temporal pressure such as dry-season concentration or intelligence alerts.

If the active asset set in zone \(i\) on day \(t\) is \(\mathcal{A}_i^t\), then detection probability is
\begin{equation}
q_i^t = 1-\prod_{a\in \mathcal{A}_i^t}(1-q_a).
\label{eq:detect}
\end{equation}
Interception depends on response time:
\begin{equation}
g_i^t = \frac{1}{1+\exp[\kappa(\tau_i^t-\tau^\star)]},
\label{eq:intercept}
\end{equation}
where \(\tau_i^t\) is realized response time and \(\tau^\star\) is the critical delay threshold.

The probability that an illegal attempt is neutralized equals
\begin{equation}
u_i^t = q_i^t g_i^t.
\end{equation}
The overall protection index is
\begin{equation}
\PI = 1-\frac{\sum_{t=1}^T \sum_i R_i L_i^t}{\sum_{t=1}^T \sum_i R_i N_i^t + \varepsilon},
\label{eq:pi}
\end{equation}
where \(L_i^t\) is the weighted successful-loss indicator.

\subsubsection{Expected Protection Index (Closed-Form Insight)}
Let \(\lambda_i^t = \lambda_0 R_i \Psi_t\). Because \(N_i^t\) is Poisson and each event is neutralized with probability \(u_i^t\), the expected loss satisfies
\[
\mathbb{E}[L_i^t] = \lambda_i^t(1-u_i^t).
\]
Taking expectations in \eqref{eq:pi} yields the interpretable approximation
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[\PI] \approx 1-\frac{\sum_{t=1}^T \sum_i R_i \lambda_i^t (1-u_i^t)}{\sum_{t=1}^T \sum_i R_i \lambda_i^t},
\label{eq:piexp}
\end{equation}
which shows that maximizing \(\PI\) is equivalent to minimizing a risk-weighted average of \(1-u_i^t\). This links the simulation outcome to the allocation objective and closes the loop between Model II and Model III.

\subsubsection{Response-Time Distribution}
Response time is modeled as a minimum travel-time base plus dispatch and terrain noise. Let \(t_{si}\) be station-to-zone travel time and \(\delta_t\) be a time-of-day dispatch delay. We model
\begin{equation}
\tau_i^t = \min_s t_{si} + \delta_t + \xi_i^t,
\label{eq:taudist}
\end{equation}
where \(\delta_t \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_\delta,\sigma_\delta^2)\) and \(\xi_i^t \sim \text{LogNormal}(\mu_\xi,\sigma_\xi^2)\) captures terrain and communication variability. This produces the right-skewed delay profile observed in field operations and aligns with the logistic interception decay in \eqref{eq:intercept}.

\subsubsection{Closed-Loop Prior Update}
Simulation outputs feed back into the risk prior used by Model I. We use a stable update that blends patrol pressure and local incident signals:
\begin{equation}
C_i^{t+1}=(1-\omega)C_i^t+\omega\left(\frac{F_i^t}{\max_k F_k^t}\right), \qquad
P_i^{t+1}=\sigma(\theta_0+\theta_1A_i+\theta_2B_i-\theta_3C_i^{t+1}),
\label{eq:priorupdate}
\end{equation}
where \(\sigma(\cdot)\) is the logistic function. Allocation increases \(C_i\), which suppresses \(P_i\), which then reshapes the next optimization in the rolling horizon. This closes the risk--deployment--simulation loop.

\subsubsection{Monotonicity of Protection with Effort}
Holding arrivals fixed, the model is monotone in effort: increasing patrol intensity \(F_i\), devices \(y_i\), or UAV coverage \(z_j\) weakly increases \(\eta_i\), which increases \(q_i^t\) and therefore \(u_i^t\). Since \(\PI\) is decreasing in the weighted loss numerator, \(\PI\) is non-decreasing in any effort variable. This guarantees that the optimization and simulation layers are directionally consistent and prevents pathological feedback in the rolling horizon.

\subsection{Operational Scheduling}
The simulation uses staggered patrol departures, continuous device monitoring, and twice-daily drone windows. This structure matters because identical total effort can perform very differently when attention is synchronized instead of distributed across the day. Figure~\ref{fig:respmap} reveals the station-centered response isochrones and the eastern delay pocket; Figure~\ref{fig:schedule} shows the 24-hour command cycle used in the baseline plan.

This is consistent with empirical evidence that patrol effectiveness can vary widely by how patrol effort is organized and where patrol paths are concentrated \citep{usgsDetection}.

\subsubsection{Spatiotemporal Deployment Recommendations}
Two management findings stand out. First, strategic concentration dominates broad but shallow coverage: the top-risk quartile contains 57\% of the biological value but only 25\% of the area, so partial saturation of these zones outperforms weak coverage everywhere. Second, mixed assets outperform patrol-only protection because fixed devices raise early warning while drones reduce response uncertainty.

From these results, we recommend a three-part policy package for Etosha-like parks:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Priority zoning policy}: formally designate the top-risk quartile as a recurrent high-attention layer with minimum revisit constraints.
  \item \textbf{Hybrid surveillance policy}: reserve fixed devices for persistent hotspots and use drones as a dynamic uncertainty-reduction layer, not as patrol substitutes.
  \item \textbf{Staffing floor policy}: maintain at least 34 field rangers under baseline detection quality; below this threshold, protection degradation accelerates nonlinearly.
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:simulation} shows that the optimized strategy crosses the command target of \(\PI=0.75\) after roughly one week and remains stable under stochastic pressure, whereas the uniform strategy never reaches that threshold. Figure~\ref{fig:robustness} shows the same advantage statistically: the optimized distribution shifts rightward and thins the low-performance tail across replications.

\subsubsection{Feasibility, Staffing Requirement, and Action Package}
To ensure the recommended allocation is operationally executable, we verify two constraints with baseline staffing:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Shift feasibility}: \(34\) field rangers provide \(34\times 8 = 272\) person-hours/day. With assumed field utilization \(\rho=0.78\), usable effort is about \(212\) person-hours/day.
  \item \textbf{Route feasibility}: the selected route package requires approximately \(198\) person-hours/day in expectation, leaving a small reserve for incident response and weather delays.
\end{itemize}
To maintain \(\PI \ge 0.70\), the minimum field staffing level is estimated by
\begin{equation}
n_{\min} = \left\lceil \frac{\sum_{i\in \mathcal{H}} f_i^\star \bar{\tau}_i}{h_{\text{shift}}\rho} \right\rceil,
\label{eq:rangermin}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{H}\) is the high-risk zone set, \(f_i^\star\) is the required revisit frequency, \(h_{\text{shift}}\) is usable field time per ranger, and \(\rho\) is field utilization. Under the baseline technology mix, Equation~\eqref{eq:rangermin} and the Monte Carlo outputs agree on a minimum of about 34 field rangers (17 two-person patrol teams). Hence the baseline strategy is not only optimal in the model, but also schedulable in routine operations.
