\section{Service Reliability, Queueing Envelope, and Reserve Sizing}
\subsection{Why Staffing Must Be Derived from Service Commitments}
A protection plan can look strong in spatial terms and still fail because the busy stations cannot serve their assigned hinges quickly enough. For that reason, the staffing problem is not treated here as a patrol-hours bookkeeping exercise. It is treated as a service-reliability problem in which each station must keep delay exceedance below a target threshold.

\subsection{Detection and Effective Arrival Pressure}
The barrier design does not eliminate all pressure, so the service layer starts from the residual debt field and converts it into station-facing arrival intensity. If the active asset set in cell \(i\) is \(\mathcal{A}_i\), then detection follows the familiar fusion rule
\begin{equation}
q_i = 1-\prod_{a\in\mathcal{A}_i}(1-q_a).
\label{eq:detect}
\end{equation}
Cells are then assigned to the station most likely to serve them. For station \(s\), the residual arrival pressure is aggregated as
\begin{equation}
\lambda_s = \lambda_0 \sum_{i\in\mathcal{Z}_s} \Gamma_i(1-q_i),
\label{eq:arrival}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{Z}_s\) is the set of cells effectively dependent on station \(s\). This moves the staffing logic from abstract park-wide averages to the actual station bottlenecks.

\subsection{Queueing Reliability}
Let \(c_s\) be the number of two-person teams assigned to station \(s\), and let \(\mu_s\) be its mean service rate. The traffic intensity is
\[
\rho_s=\frac{\lambda_s}{c_s\mu_s}.
\]
Under an \(M/M/c_s\) approximation, the Erlang-C waiting probability is
\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{wait},s}=
\frac{\dfrac{(c_s\rho_s)^{c_s}}{c_s!(1-\rho_s)}}
{\sum_{k=0}^{c_s-1}\dfrac{(c_s\rho_s)^k}{k!}+\dfrac{(c_s\rho_s)^{c_s}}{c_s!(1-\rho_s)}},
\label{eq:erlang}
\end{equation}
and the probability that service exceeds the commitment threshold \(\tau^\star\) is
\begin{equation}
\Pr(T_s>\tau^\star)=
P_{\mathrm{wait},s}\exp\!\left[-(c_s\mu_s-\lambda_s)\bigl(\tau^\star-\mu_s^{-1}\bigr)_+\right].
\label{eq:intercept}
\end{equation}
This gives the design an explicit service envelope instead of an average-delay heuristic.

\subsection{Cell-Level Service Success and Protection Index}
Each cell inherits the service reliability of its dominant station. If \(s(i)\) is the serving station for cell \(i\), then
\begin{equation}
g_i = 1-\Pr(T_{s(i)}>\tau^\star).
\label{eq:service}
\end{equation}
The park-wide protection index becomes
\begin{equation}
\PI = 1-\frac{\sum_i \Gamma_i(1-q_i g_i)}{\sum_i \Gamma_i+\varepsilon}.
\label{eq:pi}
\end{equation}
In this formulation, \(\PI\) is the complement of residual weighted loss after both monitoring and service reliability are accounted for.

\subsection{Reserve Dispatch Logic}
The mobile reserve should not be permanently pre-allocated to one sector. It should be sent where it most reduces delay exceedance. Define the marginal benefit of adding one temporary service unit to station \(s\) as
\begin{equation}
\Delta_s = \Pr(T_s>\tau^\star\mid c_s)-\Pr(T_s>\tau^\star\mid c_s+1).
\label{eq:dispatchscore}
\end{equation}
The UAV-assisted reserve is therefore assigned to the sector tied to the station with the largest weighted value \(\lambda_s \Delta_s\). This is a queueing-based dispatch rule rather than a pure hotspot rule.

\subsection{Service Calendar}
The park still operates on staggered patrol departures, continuous sentinel watch, and dedicated aerial windows. What changes is the logic used to time those windows: busy stations with the highest marginal reduction in \(\Pr(T>\tau^\star)\) receive reserve support first.

\subsection{Staffing Floor}
The staffing floor is now derived from service commitments rather than from average patrol effort. The minimum feasible ranger count is
\begin{equation}
n_{\min}
=
2\sum_s
\min\Bigl\{
c_s:\Pr(T_s>\tau^\star)\le \epsilon_s
\Bigr\},
\label{eq:rangermin}
\end{equation}
where \(\epsilon_s\) is the acceptable delay-exceedance level for station \(s\). Under baseline assumptions, this lands at approximately 34 field rangers. The constraint comes from service congestion at critical stations rather than from route-hours alone.

\subsection{Interpretation}
Two conclusions follow. First, Etosha behaves like a hinge system, not like a uniform corridor. Second, staffing should be protected because the main nonlinear failure occurs when one or two stations become congested and can no longer honor the response commitment for high-debt cells.
