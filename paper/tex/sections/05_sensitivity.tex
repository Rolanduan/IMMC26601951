\section{Stress Testing and Safe Operating Envelope}
The controller is only useful if managers know how it fails. We therefore stress test four major levers: ranger count, device inventory, patrol cadence, and effective detection quality. The purpose is not to rank parameters mechanically; it is to identify the boundary between stable and fragile protection.

We define the safe operating envelope as
\[
\mathcal{E}=\left\{(n,q,\Delta): \PI \ge 0.70,\ \Resp \le \tau^\star,\ \Det \ge 0.60 \right\},
\]
where \(n\) is field staffing, \(q\) is effective detection quality, and \(\Delta\) is patrol interval. Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity} ranks the levers, Figure~\ref{fig:rangercurve} shows the staffing breakpoint, and Figure~\ref{fig:scenariomatrix} maps the manpower--detection frontier. The lever ranking is
\[
\text{ranger count} > \text{detection quality} > \text{patrol interval} > \text{device inventory}.
\]

\subsection{Staffing Shock}
Reducing field rangers from 34 to 24 pushes the system out of the safe envelope: \(\PI\) falls from about 0.74 to 0.58. This is not a linear decline. Once hotspot revisit discipline breaks, the system loses repeated service in the very cells that carry the most weighted consequence.

\subsection{Monitoring Reduction}
Reducing fixed devices from 12 to 6 lowers \(\PI\) from 0.74 to 0.68. The drop is noticeable but smaller than the staffing shock because the controller already places devices in the highest-value persistent hotspots. After those sites are instrumented, extra devices add progressively less value.

\subsection{Cadence Drift}
Increasing the mean patrol interval from 1.5 to 3.0 days weakens deterrence continuity and raises the probability that hotspot cells spend long periods without active service. This matters most in zones with high accessibility, where adversaries can exploit predictable quiet windows.

\subsection{Detection Perturbation}
Increasing effective zone-level detection quality from 0.40 to 0.60 yields a protection gain comparable to adding roughly 6--8 field rangers. This does not mean technology replaces staff. It means that intelligence quality, placement discipline, and monitoring uptime are the most credible partial substitutes once staffing is fixed.

Using Equation~\eqref{eq:rangermin}, the baseline ranger floor is about 34 field rangers. If devices are cut by one-third, the requirement rises to about 40. If devices increase by 50\%, it falls to about 29. Taken together, the stress tests define a clear management lesson: protect staffing first, protect detection quality second, and treat devices as reinforcement rather than as the primary backbone of control.
