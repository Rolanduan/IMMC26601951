\section{From Hotspot Maps to Ecological-Liability Contracts}
\subsection{Why Coverage Language Is Too Weak}
Protected areas are often evaluated as if all land parcels contributed equally to conservation failure and as if the main task of management were simply to maximize visible patrol presence. That framing is operationally weak. Real losses occur when a biologically costly event happens in a zone that is both hard to observe and hard to serve. The correct unit of decision is therefore not land area, and not even a hotspot by itself, but an \emph{ecological-liability cell}: a place where biological consequence, incident pressure, information uncertainty, and service drag accumulate into one costly exposure \citep{watson2014,geldmann2013,coad2019,jones2018}.

Etosha National Park is a particularly clear case. Water dependence concentrates wildlife value, access is highly uneven, and station geometry makes some cells systematically harder to serve than others \citep{etoshaOfficial,etoshaEcology}. Those properties imply that management quality should be judged by how much ecological liability remains unresolved after assets are deployed, not by how many route kilometers appear on a weekly map.

\subsection{Problem Restatement}
We therefore restate the IMMC problem as follows: design a protection contract that minimizes unresolved ecological liability under limited ranger time, limited persistent sensors, limited aerial reserve, and uncertain daily operating conditions.

The park state at time \(t\) is written as
\[
\mathcal{X}_t = \bigl(L_t,U_t,W_t\bigr),
\]
where \(L_t\) is the liability field, \(U_t\) the information-uncertainty field, and \(W_t\) the station workload state. A deployment decision
\[
a_t=(x_t,y_t,z_t)
\]
specifies patrol-template activations, sentinel-device placements, and UAV reserve windows. The management target is to minimize unresolved liability while ensuring that the most expensive cells receive a guaranteed minimum protection quality and that station overload remains below a stated service covenant.

\subsection{New Analytic Spine}
To operationalize that restatement, we use an architecture that is structurally different from plain hotspot ranking:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item a \textbf{GIS-grounded ecological-liability field} that estimates where high biological consequence and high operating uncertainty coexist;
  \item an \textbf{ambiguity-aware protection portfolio} that minimizes expected loss and the tail risk of bad scenarios;
  \item a \textbf{workload-guarantee layer} that sizes staffing from overload probabilities rather than from average patrol hours;
  \item a \textbf{managerial contract layer} that converts the mathematics into enforceable field rules.
\end{enumerate}

The information flow is
\[
\text{basemap + ecology + access + stations}
\;\longrightarrow\;
\text{liability field + contract cells}
\;\longrightarrow\;
\text{portfolio design under ambiguity}
\;\longrightarrow\;
\text{workload guarantee and reserve recourse}.
\]
Figure~\ref{fig:system} summarizes this new pipeline visually. The central object is no longer a corridor to be sealed, but a liability contract to be honored.

\subsection{Management Metrics}
The new framework is evaluated by three deliberately different metrics:
\begin{align}
\PI &: \text{protection index, i.e., the fraction of baseline ecological liability that is resolved}, \\
\operatorname{CVaR}_{0.9}(Y) &: \text{tail risk of unresolved liability under bad operating scenarios}, \\
\Pr(B_s>b^\star) &: \text{station-overload probability relative to a backlog tolerance } b^\star.
\end{align}
These indicators force the paper to remain operational. A design is not acceptable if it performs well on average but leaves the upper tail of loss too large or drives one station into recurrent overload.
