\section{Systems Framing and Decision Logic}
\subsection{Why Large-Park Protection Must Be Treated as a System}
Protected areas are often evaluated as if legal designation and spatial extent were enough to imply protection. The empirical literature shows otherwise. Conservation outcomes depend heavily on operational capacity, local pressure, and management quality \citep{watson2014,geldmann2013}. Many reserves remain biodiversity-critical on paper while being practically under-protected on the ground because staffing, monitoring, and response systems are too weak relative to the pressure they face \citep{coad2019,jones2018}. In that setting, the core problem is not merely ecological description; it is operational design.

Anti-poaching work makes this especially clear. Patrol effectiveness varies substantially across space, route geometry, team behavior, and deployment discipline \citep{moore2018,usgsDetection}. Illegal actors adapt to access corridors, surveillance gaps, and predictable schedules. As a result, protection performance cannot be inferred from total patrol hours alone. It depends on whether scarce effort is concentrated in the right places, on the right cadence, with enough response capacity to turn detection into interception.

Etosha National Park is an instructive case because it exposes this systems problem sharply. Wildlife aggregates around water sources, the park contains long east--west operating corridors, and response times differ meaningfully across stations and sectors \citep{etoshaOfficial,etoshaEcology}. Those characteristics produce an uneven landscape of risk, observability, and controllability. In practical terms, managers are not choosing whether to patrol; they are choosing which zones receive reliable protective service and which zones accept residual risk.

\subsection{Problem Restatement as a Control Problem}
The IMMC problem therefore requires more than a descriptive hotspot map. It requires a deployable operating logic that can identify decisive zones, allocate multiple protection assets under operational constraints, evaluate performance over time, estimate feasible staffing requirements, and transfer to parks with different ecological and mobility structures. That is a coupled estimation--allocation--simulation problem under uncertainty.

We write this as a control architecture. Let
\[
s_t = (R^t,P^t,\tau^t)
\]
denote the park state at time \(t\), including the spatial risk field, latent intrusion pressure, and response-time structure. Let
\[
u_t = (x_t,y_t,z_t)
\]
denote the deployment decision composed of patrol-loop activations, fixed-device assignments, and drone sorties. The operating system evolves under exogenous uncertainty \(\omega_t\) and observed field signals \(o_t\):
\[
s_{t+1}=f(s_t,u_t,\omega_t), \qquad o_t=h(s_t,u_t).
\]
The management task is to choose \(u_t\) so that high-risk zones remain above an acceptable protection standard while respecting manpower, mobility, and equipment constraints.

This framing matters because it changes what counts as a good solution. A good solution is not the one that paints the entire map as ``covered.'' It is the one that maintains service reliability in decisive zones, degrades gracefully under stress, and provides a clear update rule when conditions change.

\subsection{Paper Architecture}
To make that control problem operational, we build a protected-area operating system with three analytic cores and one implementation layer:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item a GIS digital twin that converts map structure into zone-level ecological, access, and response features;
  \item a spatial priority engine that estimates where protective service is most valuable and most vulnerable;
  \item a mixed-integer controller that allocates patrols, sensors, and UAVs under real operating constraints;
  \item a stochastic validation layer that stress-tests the plan and updates the next planning cycle.
\end{enumerate}

The resulting workflow is
\[
\mathcal{D}_t \xrightarrow{\text{digital twin}} \mathcal{S}_t
\xrightarrow{\text{priority engine}} R_i^t
\xrightarrow{\text{controller}} u_t^\star=(x_t^\star,y_t^\star,z_t^\star)
\xrightarrow{\text{stress test}} \PI_t.
\]
This architecture combines spatial modeling, optimization, and stochastic simulation in a single decision loop \citep{cressie1993,anselin1995,diggle2013,dantzig1963,birge2011,law2015,banks2010,bonabeau2002,macal2010}. It is designed to support both competition-grade analysis and field-style management reasoning.

\subsection{Operating Principles}
Figure~\ref{fig:system} shows the protected-area operating system as a closed loop from state estimation to deployment and governance. The architecture is built on three operating principles:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Selective saturation}: high-risk zones should receive repeated, reliable service before low-risk zones receive shallow coverage.
  \item \textbf{Feasible control}: every recommended plan must be executable under shift limits, station capacity, travel time, and equipment inventory.
  \item \textbf{Adaptive updating}: patrol pressure, detections, and delays must feed back into the next planning cycle instead of being treated as static background information.
\end{itemize}

We evaluate each strategy with three management-facing metrics:
\begin{align}
\PI &: \text{risk-weighted protection index (higher is better)}, \\
\Det &: \text{mean detection probability across active zones}, \\
\Resp &: \text{mean response delay in hours (lower is better)}.
\end{align}
These indicators are deliberately cross-functional: \(\PI\) captures ecological consequence, \(\Det\) captures surveillance quality, and \(\Resp\) captures operational friction.
