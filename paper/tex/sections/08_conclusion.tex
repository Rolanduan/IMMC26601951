\section{Conclusion}
The conservation problem in large parks is fundamentally one of strategic selectivity under uncertainty. Our solution therefore rejects full-coverage planning and instead builds a three-model core framework embedded in a decision-to-deployment pipeline, so risk diagnosis, constrained allocation, and dynamic validation remain operationally consistent over time.

For Etosha, the framework recommends concentrating effort around waterhole-access corridors, combining patrols with fixed devices and drones, and maintaining approximately 34 field rangers to sustain a strong protection level under baseline technology. The same structure can be transferred to other parks with only data-layer recalibration. This makes the model suitable not only for competition analysis, but also for practical decision support in conservation management.

Direct answers to Tasks 1--6, with Task 7 delivered in the front-matter letter, are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Task 1: prioritize the eastern--southern hotspot corridor and define protection through recurrent attention, detection probability, and response delay.
  \item Task 2: allocate patrol loops first, then fixed devices to persistent hotspots, then drones to uncertainty-heavy sectors.
  \item Task 3: maintain at least 34 field rangers (17 teams) for \(\PI \ge 0.70\) under baseline detection conditions.
  \item Task 4: manpower and detection quality are the dominant sensitivity levers.
  \item Task 5: the model is interpretable and operationally aligned, with threat-prior calibration as the key limitation.
  \item Task 6: the architecture transfers unchanged; ecological and mobility data layers must be recalibrated by park.
\end{itemize}
