\section{Decision Synthesis}
The central conclusion of this paper is that large-park protection should be run as an operating system, not as a coverage checklist. Etosha is too large, too heterogeneous, and too operationally uneven for uniform patrol logic to work. The decisive management problem is to guarantee reliable service in zones where ecological consequence, intrusion opportunity, and response friction coincide.

The proposed system solves that problem through a single closed loop: build the digital twin, estimate the priority field, allocate patrols and technology under real constraints, stress test the result under uncertainty, and update the next plan from observed pressure and delay. In the Etosha baseline, this logic supports a corridor-focused deployment, lifts the protection index from 0.56 to 0.74, and identifies a minimum staffing threshold of about 34 field rangers.

Direct answers to Tasks 1--6 are therefore:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Task 1: define protection as reliable service in decisive zones and prioritize the eastern--southern corridor.
  \item Task 2: allocate patrol loops as the backbone, fixed devices for persistence, and UAVs for uncertainty reduction and surge support.
  \item Task 3: maintain roughly 34 field rangers under baseline conditions to keep the system in its safe operating envelope.
  \item Task 4: recognize staffing as the dominant performance lever, with detection quality second.
  \item Task 5: treat stale threat priors and oversimplified mobility assumptions as the most important model risks.
  \item Task 6: transfer the architecture directly while recalibrating ecological, threat, and mobility layers locally.
\end{itemize}
