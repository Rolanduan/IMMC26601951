\section{Ecological-Liability Surface and GIS Diagnosis}
Etosha should not be read as a flat field of independent hotspot scores. It should be read as a heterogeneous liability surface in which some cells are expensive because of ecology, others because of intrusion pressure, and still others because uncertainty and delayed service magnify the cost of any failure. The purpose of the diagnostic layer is to put those forces on the same scale.

\subsection{Grid and State Variables}
We partition the park into an \(8\times 8\) management lattice. Each cell \(i\) stores four strategic objects:
\[
V_i:\text{ biological consequence},\qquad
\lambda_i:\text{ latent incident intensity},\qquad
U_i:\text{ information deficit},\qquad
D_i:\text{ service drag}.
\]
All map plates in this section are anchored to the official Etosha basemap and upgraded with GIS overlays so that the final diagnosis remains geographically interpretable.

\subsection{Latent Incident Intensity}
Instead of assuming that pressure is known directly, we estimate a latent incident field with graph-regularized smoothing. Let \(\widetilde \ell_i\) denote a raw linear predictor built from access and terrain features:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde \ell_i = \beta_0+\beta_1A_i+\beta_2G_i+\beta_3R_i,
\label{eq:rawfield}
\end{equation}
where \(A_i\) is access convenience, \(G_i\) is gate or boundary exposure, and \(R_i\) is road-linked reach. Let \(L_G\) be the graph Laplacian of the adjacency graph on the management lattice. We then solve
\begin{equation}
\ell^\star
=
\arg\min_{\ell\in\mathbb{R}^{64}}
\left\{
\sum_{i=1}^{64}\omega_i(\ell_i-\widetilde \ell_i)^2
\;+\;
\rho\,\ell^\top L_G \ell
\right\},
\label{eq:prior}
\end{equation}
and define the latent incident intensity as
\begin{equation}
\lambda_i = \exp(\ell_i^\star).
\label{eq:intensity}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:prior} is not a transport-flow model. It is a field-estimation model: access-based predictors are shrunk toward a spatially coherent intensity surface before the protection decision begins.

\subsection{Biological Consequence}
The ecological cost of failure is handled separately:
\begin{equation}
V_i = 0.45M_i + 0.35H_i + 0.20B_i,
\label{eq:value}
\end{equation}
where \(M_i\) is wildlife concentration, \(H_i\) is water dependence, and \(B_i\) is species sensitivity. This term answers the question ``How costly is a failure here?'' and intentionally remains distinct from incident intensity.

\subsection{Information Deficit}
The third ingredient is not threat but ignorance. Cells that are rarely revisited or hard to observe should receive an ambiguity premium. Let \(r_i\) denote monitoring recency in days and \(\kappa_i\in[0,1]\) denote sightline quality. We define the information deficit by
\begin{equation}
U_i = \frac{1}{1+r_i} + \chi(1-\kappa_i).
\label{eq:criticality}
\end{equation}
The first term penalizes stale information; the second penalizes poor natural observability. High \(U_i\) means that the park knows too little about a cell precisely when it would be expensive to know too late.

\subsection{Service Drag}
Response difficulty is carried into the diagnosis as a structural drag term rather than as a queueing calculation. Let \(d_i\) be the shortest-path travel time from the nearest station to cell \(i\). We normalize this quantity into
\begin{equation}
D_i=
\frac{d_i-\min_k d_k}
{\max_k d_k-\min_k d_k+\varepsilon},
\label{eq:response}
\end{equation}
so that \(D_i\in[0,1]\) measures how strongly geography alone penalizes timely intervention.

\subsection{Ecological Liability}
We now combine the four layers into one liability surface:
\begin{equation}
L_i
=
V_i\Bigl(
0.52\,\lambda_i
+
0.26\,U_i
+
0.22\,D_i
\Bigr).
\label{eq:risk}
\end{equation}
This quantity is intentionally different from a hotspot score. A cell becomes expensive not merely when wildlife value is high, but when value, intensity, uncertainty, and structural delay all reinforce one another.

\subsection{Contract Cells}
The top decile of the liability distribution forms the \emph{contract set}
\[
\mathcal{C}=\left\{i:\;L_i\ge q_{0.90}(L)\right\}.
\]
These are the cells on which management commits to a minimum protection standard later in the portfolio model. This shift is deliberate. Instead of vaguely saying that hotspots deserve more attention, we impose an explicit service contract on the most expensive cells.

\subsection{Etosha Diagnosis Under the New Framework}
The resulting map is not a generic east--south corridor story. It is a liability ridge. A smaller collection of eastern and south-central cells emerges because those cells combine high biological consequence with persistent uncertainty and above-average service drag. Other visually busy zones matter less once those factors are priced together.

The managerial implication is direct. Etosha should be managed by guaranteeing a minimum protection level on that ridge and then using the residual budget to reduce tail risk elsewhere.
