\section{Etosha Digital Twin and Priority Diagnosis}
Etosha should not be understood as a flat protected polygon. It behaves as a coupled ecology--access--response system. Wildlife value is not distributed uniformly, intrusion opportunity is not distributed uniformly, and the park's ability to answer an incursion is not distributed uniformly. The purpose of the digital twin is therefore to convert map structure into operationally meaningful state variables rather than to create another descriptive atlas.

\subsection{Abstraction Rules}
We make six modeling assumptions, each chosen to preserve managerial meaning while keeping the system computationally tractable.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Zonal control assumption.} The park is partitioned into management cells because decisions are made at patrol-sector scale, not at individual-animal scale.
  \item \textbf{Dry-season concentration assumption.} Water-dependent clustering is treated as the dominant driver of ecological exposure in Etosha \citep{etoshaEcology}.
  \item \textbf{Protection-service assumption.} A zone is considered well protected only when it receives recurrent attention, high detection probability, and response within the local interception window.
  \item \textbf{Latent-pressure assumption.} Intrusion pressure is only partially observed and must be estimated from access, biological value, and prior patrol pressure.
  \item \textbf{Operational-unit assumption.} Patrol teams are modeled as two-person units, and fixed devices aggregate cameras, acoustic nodes, or towers into one comparable control resource.
  \item \textbf{Feasibility-first assumption.} Operating constraints are expressed through person-hours, inventory, and sortie capacity rather than a detailed financial budget because the main management bottleneck is implementability.
\end{enumerate}

These assumptions define the digital twin as a control abstraction. The twin is not meant to mimic every ecological detail; it is meant to preserve the details that alter deployment decisions.

\subsection{Priority Logic for Etosha}
Four subsystems determine where protection effort matters most:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Ecology}: wildlife density, species sensitivity, and dry-season water dependence;
  \item \textbf{Threat exposure}: access corridors, gate-linked approach routes, and latent intrusion pressure;
  \item \textbf{Mobility}: roads, station placement, and travel-time friction;
  \item \textbf{Surveillance supply}: patrol loops, fixed sensors, and drone reach.
\end{itemize}

The interaction of these subsystems produces the decisive Etosha management insight: the highest-priority zones are not simply those with the most animals. They are the zones where ecological value, exploitable access, and slow response overlap. In system terms, those are the cells with the largest expected loss under a service failure.

\subsection{Protection as Reliable Service}
We therefore define wildlife protection as reliable service delivery in high-consequence cells. A zone is effectively protected when three conditions are met simultaneously:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item conservation value is high enough that failure matters;
  \item surveillance is strong enough that illegal activity is likely to be detected;
  \item response is fast enough that detection can still be converted into interception.
\end{enumerate}

This definition is stronger than a simple patrol-presence metric. It forces the model to connect ecology, detection, and response within the same decision language, which is necessary for a park as spatially uneven as Etosha.

\subsection{Spatial Priority Engine}
\subsubsection{Cell Representation}
We discretize the park into an \(8\times 8\) management grid, producing 64 zones. Each zone \(i\) stores six core features:
\[
W_i:\text{ wildlife density},\quad
S_i:\text{ species sensitivity},\quad
H_i:\text{ waterhole proximity},
\]
\[
A_i:\text{ accessibility},\quad
D_i:\text{ response difficulty},\quad
P_i:\text{ latent intrusion prior}.
\]
The grid is coarse enough to remain interpretable to managers and fine enough to preserve the eastern corridor, southern road system, Etosha Pan, and station geometry.

\subsubsection{Response Friction from the Mobility Layer}
Let \(t_{si}\) be the travel time from station \(s\) to zone \(i\) along the road network. Response difficulty is derived from the fastest feasible travel time and normalized to \([0,1]\):
\begin{equation}
D_i = \frac{\min_s t_{si}-\min_k \min_s t_{sk}}{\max_k \min_s t_{sk}-\min_k \min_s t_{sk}+\varepsilon}.
\label{eq:response}
\end{equation}
This step is important because it converts geography into controllability. A remote but biologically important zone becomes operationally fragile if the park cannot answer an incident there quickly.

\subsubsection{Value, Exposure, and Composite Risk}
We separate ecological consequence from operational exposure. The value score is
\begin{align}
B_i &= 0.45W_i + 0.35H_i + 0.20S_i,
\end{align}
while the threat-exposure score is
\begin{align}
T_i &= 0.40A_i + 0.30D_i + 0.30P_i.
\end{align}
The composite priority score becomes
\begin{equation}
R_i = 0.55B_i + 0.45T_i.
\label{eq:risk}
\end{equation}

The weighting intentionally gives a slight edge to ecological value because this is a conservation controller, not a pure crime-suppression model. A zone is prioritized because losing it matters ecologically, not merely because it is easy to exploit.

\subsubsection{Latent Intrusion Prior}
Intrusion pressure is represented through a logistic prior:
\begin{equation}
P_i = \frac{1}{1+\exp[-(\theta_0+\theta_1 A_i+\theta_2 B_i-\theta_3 C_i)]},
\label{eq:prior}
\end{equation}
where \(C_i\) is recent protection pressure. This formulation captures a key behavioral effect: attractive, accessible zones become more dangerous when patrol pressure relaxes, and some pressure is displaced away from heavily serviced cells.

Let \(\Omega_i = \log\left(\frac{P_i}{1-P_i}\right)\) be the log-odds of intrusion. Then
\begin{equation}
\Omega_i = \theta_0+\theta_1 A_i+\theta_2 B_i-\theta_3 C_i.
\label{eq:logodds}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:logodds} gives the prior a direct management interpretation: access and biological reward increase pressure, while sustained patrol pressure suppresses it.

\subsubsection{Etosha Priority Diagnosis}
The highest-risk quartile forms a corridor rather than a random scatter. The east and south dominate because three forces align there: wildlife concentration near water, relatively easy access, and slower response from key stations. The western interior is less exposed because access is harder and wildlife concentration is more diffuse. The Pan remains ecologically prominent but is less attractive for covert extraction because it is open and operationally conspicuous.

This diagnosis is the first major system output. It tells managers that Etosha should be run as a corridor-defense problem with supporting flank coverage, not as an equal-area patrol problem.
