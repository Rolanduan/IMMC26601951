\section{Operational Control and Resource Allocation}
\subsection{Decision Layers}
Once the priority field is known, the next question is not simply how much effort to spend, but how to structure that effort. We distinguish three control layers:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{anchoring}: repeatable patrol loops that guarantee physical presence in decisive cells;
  \item \textbf{persistence}: fixed devices that maintain continuous watch in stable hotspots;
  \item \textbf{adaptation}: drone sorties that absorb uncertainty and provide surge observation.
\end{itemize}
Those three layers become the decision variables of the controller:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \(x_r \in \mathbb{Z}_{+}\): number of times patrol loop \(r\) is activated;
  \item \(y_i \in \{0,1,2\}\): number of fixed devices assigned to zone \(i\);
  \item \(z_j \in \mathbb{Z}_{+}\): drone sorties assigned to aerial sector \(j\).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Route Library and Patrol Geometry}
\subsubsection{Feasible Loop Construction}
Let \(\mathcal{R}\) be the patrol-loop library generated from stations, roads, and time budgets. Each candidate loop must start and end at a station, traverse connected zones, and satisfy
\begin{equation}
h_r = \frac{L_r}{v} + t_{\text{stop}} \le h_{\max},
\label{eq:route}
\end{equation}
where \(L_r\) is loop length, \(v\) is patrol speed, and \(t_{\text{stop}}\) captures observation and reporting time. This prevents the controller from recommending routes that are mathematically attractive but operationally impossible.

The zone-coverage matrix is defined by
\begin{equation}
a_{ir} =
\begin{cases}
1, & \text{if route } r \text{ traverses zone } i,\\
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
Patrol intensity in zone \(i\) is therefore
\begin{equation}
F_i = \sum_{r\in\mathcal{R}} a_{ir}x_r.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Enumeration and Pruning}
The road network is represented as a weighted graph \(\mathcal{G}=(V,E)\) with travel-time weights. For each station, we generate \(K\)-shortest candidate paths to high-value waypoints and then form loops by concatenating outbound and return paths. We prune the resulting library by:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item removing any route that violates the time bound \eqref{eq:route};
  \item removing dominated routes whose zone set is a strict subset of another lower-cost route;
  \item limiting excessive overlap among routes from the same station so that the library preserves tactical variety.
\end{enumerate}
This produces a controller-ready route set that behaves like an operational menu rather than an arbitrary mathematical index.

\subsection{Protection Gain Model}
The controller values assets for their contribution to service intensity. Let
\[
\eta_i=\alpha F_i+\beta y_i+\gamma\sum_j b_{ij}z_j
\]
denote total surveillance intensity in zone \(i\), where \(b_{ij}=1\) if drone sector \(j\) covers zone \(i\). Zone-level protection gain is modeled as
\begin{equation}
E_i = 1-\exp\!\left(-\alpha F_i-\beta y_i-\gamma \sum_j b_{ij}z_j\right).
\label{eq:gain}
\end{equation}
This is a deliberately saturating response: the first patrol, first device, and first drone window matter much more than later increments.

The gain model can be derived from an event-level view. If illegal arrivals in zone \(i\) are Poisson with mean \(\lambda_i\), then expected prevented weighted loss in one planning period is
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[\Delta_i]=R_i\lambda_i\left(1-e^{-\eta_i}\right)\bar{g}_i,
\label{eq:delta}
\end{equation}
where \(\bar{g}_i\) is expected interception success conditional on detection.

\begin{proposition}
For fixed \(\bar{g}_i\), the protection-gain function \(E_i(\eta_i)=1-e^{-\eta_i}\) is increasing and strictly concave.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
\[
\frac{\partial E_i}{\partial \eta_i}=e^{-\eta_i}>0,\qquad
\frac{\partial^2 E_i}{\partial \eta_i^2}=-e^{-\eta_i}<0.
\]
The controller therefore prefers concentrated reinforcement in decisive cells but recognizes diminishing marginal returns.
\end{proof}

\subsection{MILP Approximation and Control Insight}
Because the solver is mixed-integer linear, we use a capped linear surrogate:
\[
e_i = \min\{1,\alpha F_i+\beta y_i+\gamma\sum_j b_{ij}z_j\}.
\]
The implementation enforces
\begin{align}
e_i &\le \alpha F_i+\beta y_i+\gamma\sum_j b_{ij}z_j, \label{eq:lin1}\\
e_i &\le 1. \label{eq:lin2}
\end{align}
which preserves monotonicity and tractability while remaining close to the original concave response.

Ignoring integrality and hotspot floors, the continuous relaxation equalizes weighted marginal value across active zones:
\begin{equation}
R_i e^{-\eta_i} \approx R_k e^{-\eta_k}.
\label{eq:balancing}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:balancing} is the core allocation insight. Scarce resources are pulled toward cells with large \(R_i\) until their marginal value is driven down to the same level as other active cells. That is why the optimized strategy concentrates along the eastern--southern corridor instead of dispersing effort uniformly.

\subsection{Controller Objective and Constraints}
The optimization model maximizes weighted expected protection net of route burden:
\begin{equation}
\max \; Z = \sum_{i=1}^{64} R_iE_i - \lambda \sum_{r\in\mathcal{R}} h_rx_r,
\label{eq:obj}
\end{equation}
with \(E_i\) replaced by the linearized surrogate \(e_i\) in implementation.

The controller is subject to five feasibility rules:
\begin{align}
\sum_{r\in\mathcal{R}} 2h_r x_r &\le H_R, \label{eq:rangerhours}\\
\sum_{i=1}^{64} y_i &\le M, \label{eq:devices}\\
\sum_j z_j &\le U, \label{eq:drones}\\
\sum_{r: s(r)=s} x_r &\le \kappa_s, \quad \forall s, \label{eq:station}\\
F_i + y_i + \sum_j b_{ij}z_j &\ge \underline{q}\,\mathbb{I}(R_i\ge q_{0.75}),
\label{eq:hotspots}
\end{align}
where \(H_R\) is total ranger-hours, \(M\) is device inventory, \(U\) is available drone sorties, and \(\kappa_s\) is station capacity. The hotspot-floor constraint \eqref{eq:hotspots} is the mathematical expression of the operating doctrine: decisive cells must not be allowed to fall below a minimum service level.

\subsection{Staffing Floor and Rolling-Horizon Control}
If \(\mathcal{H}\) is the hotspot set and \(f_i^\star\) is the required revisit frequency in those cells, then a conservative staffing floor is
\begin{equation}
n_{\min} \ge \frac{\sum_{i\in\mathcal{H}} f_i^\star \bar{h}_i}{h_{\text{shift}}\rho},
\label{eq:rangermin2}
\end{equation}
where \(\bar{h}_i\) is the representative route-hours required to protect hotspot \(i\), \(h_{\text{shift}}\) is usable shift time, and \(\rho\) is field utilization. This equation closes the loop between hotspot protection and staffing demand.

\subsection{Baseline Control Package}
Under baseline assumptions, the controller assigns 49\% of patrol-hours to the eastern sector, 31\% to the southern--central corridor, and 20\% to western and northern sectors. Eight of the twelve fixed devices are anchored in the top-risk quartile, while four drone sorties are rotated across three high-uncertainty sectors. This is not a cosmetic rebalance; it is a structural shift from thin park-wide patrol coverage to corridor-based service assurance.
