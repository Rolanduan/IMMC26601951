\section{Resource Allocation Model}
\subsection{Decision Variables}
We build a route-based mixed-integer allocation model. Let \(\mathcal{R}\) be a library of feasible patrol loops generated from ranger stations and roads. Decision variables are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \(x_r \in \mathbb{Z}_{+}\): number of times route \(r\) is activated in the planning horizon;
  \item \(y_i \in \{0,1,2\}\): number of fixed monitoring devices assigned to zone \(i\);
  \item \(z_j \in \mathbb{Z}_{+}\): drone sorties allocated to aerial sector \(j\).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Route Library Construction and Coverage Matrices}
Each candidate patrol loop \(r\) is a closed path that starts and ends at a station, visits a connected set of zones, and respects the daily time budget:
\begin{equation}
h_r = \frac{L_r}{v} + t_{\text{stop}} \le h_{\max},
\label{eq:route}
\end{equation}
where \(L_r\) is loop length, \(v\) is patrol speed, and \(t_{\text{stop}}\) includes observation and report time. We generate loops by enumerating short station-to-station paths over the road graph and retaining those that satisfy \eqref{eq:route}. The coverage matrix is then
\begin{equation}
a_{ir} =
\begin{cases}
1, & \text{if route } r \text{ traverses zone } i,\\
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
This construction ensures that \(F_i=\sum_r a_{ir}x_r\) reflects feasible, schedulable loops rather than abstract coverage counts.

\subsubsection{Route Enumeration and Pruning Details}
Let the road network be a weighted graph \(\mathcal{G}=(V,E)\) with edge weights as travel time. For each station \(s\), we generate a candidate path set using the \(K\)-shortest paths from \(s\) to all other stations and major waypoints. A loop \(r\) is built by concatenating a forward path and its return, with a zone-visit list \(Z(r)\). We prune routes by:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item time feasibility: keep only paths satisfying \eqref{eq:route};
  \item coverage novelty: remove routes whose \(Z(r)\) is a strict subset of an existing route with lower \(h_r\);
  \item overlap control: limit the Jaccard overlap between routes from the same station to avoid redundant patterns.
\end{enumerate}
This yields a compact but diverse route library \(\mathcal{R}\) that captures operationally distinct patrol options.

If \(a_{ir}=1\) when route \(r\) covers zone \(i\), then patrol intensity is
\begin{equation}
F_i = \sum_{r\in\mathcal{R}} a_{ir}x_r.
\end{equation}

Expected zone protection gain is modeled as a saturating function:
\begin{equation}
E_i = 1-\exp\!\left(-\alpha F_i-\beta y_i-\gamma \sum_j b_{ij}z_j\right),
\label{eq:gain}
\end{equation}
where \(b_{ij}=1\) if sector \(j\) covers zone \(i\). Equation~\eqref{eq:gain} reflects diminishing returns: the first patrol or device matters more than the fifth.

\subsection{Derivation Logic and Structural Validity}
The optimization layer is derived from an event-level protection process. Let
\[
\eta_i=\alpha F_i+\beta y_i+\gamma\sum_j b_{ij}z_j
\]
be total surveillance intensity in zone \(i\). If illegal arrivals are Poisson with mean \(\lambda_i\), then expected prevented weighted loss in one planning period is
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[\Delta_i]=R_i\lambda_i\left(1-e^{-\eta_i}\right)\bar{g}_i,
\label{eq:delta}
\end{equation}
where \(\bar{g}_i\in(0,1)\) is expected interception success conditional on detection. Equation~\eqref{eq:delta} directly induces Equation~\eqref{eq:gain}.

\begin{proposition}
For fixed \(\bar{g}_i\), zone-level protection gain \(E_i(\eta_i)=1-e^{-\eta_i}\) is increasing and strictly concave in \(\eta_i\).
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
\[
\frac{\partial E_i}{\partial \eta_i}=e^{-\eta_i}>0,\qquad
\frac{\partial^2 E_i}{\partial \eta_i^2}=-e^{-\eta_i}<0.
\]
Hence additional resources always improve protection, but marginal gain decays with intensity.
\end{proof}

This property is crucial for policy design: under scarcity, priority zones should receive concentrated but bounded reinforcement, while low-risk zones should receive feasibility-level coverage.

The strategic-focus logic follows findings from anti-poaching security-game research: targeted, intelligence-aware deployment dominates naive uniform patrol spread \citep{fang2016paws}.

\subsubsection{Linearization Used in the MILP Solver}
The allocation model is solved as a mixed-integer linear program. We therefore use a conservative linear surrogate for the concave gain in \eqref{eq:gain}. Define
\[
e_i = \min\{1,\alpha F_i+\beta y_i+\gamma\sum_j b_{ij}z_j\}.
\]
Because \(1-e^{-\eta_i}\le \min\{1,\eta_i\}\) for all \(\eta_i\ge 0\), the linear cap is an upper envelope of the exact gain. The MILP implementation enforces
\begin{align}
e_i &\le \alpha F_i+\beta y_i+\gamma\sum_j b_{ij}z_j, \label{eq:lin1}\\
e_i &\le 1. \label{eq:lin2}
\end{align}
This preserves monotonicity and concavity while keeping the optimization linear and reproducible. The resulting allocation is conservative: if a plan is optimal under the linear cap, it remains feasible and close to optimal under the true concave response. This is the same linearization used in the reference implementation in Appendix A.

\subsubsection{Continuous Relaxation Insight}
Ignoring integrality and the hotspot floor, the continuous relaxation is a concave program. The stationarity condition implies a balancing rule: for any two zones \(i,k\) that receive positive marginal allocation,
\begin{equation}
R_i e^{-\eta_i} \approx R_k e^{-\eta_k}.
\label{eq:balancing}
\end{equation}
The optimizer therefore equalizes weighted marginal gains across active zones. This explains why the corridor concentrates resources under scarcity: only zones with large \(R_i\) can justify larger \(\eta_i\).

\subsection{Objective and Constraints}
The allocation model maximizes weighted expected protection:
\begin{equation}
\max \; Z = \sum_{i=1}^{64} R_iE_i - \lambda \sum_{r\in\mathcal{R}} h_rx_r,
\label{eq:obj}
\end{equation}
where \(h_r\) is the hours required by route \(r\).
In the MILP implementation we substitute \(E_i\) with the linearized utility \(e_i\) in \eqref{eq:lin1}--\eqref{eq:lin2}.

The main feasibility constraints are
\begin{align}
\sum_{r\in\mathcal{R}} 2h_r x_r &\le H_R, \label{eq:rangerhours}\\
\sum_{i=1}^{64} y_i &\le M, \label{eq:devices}\\
\sum_j z_j &\le U, \label{eq:drones}\\
\sum_{r: s(r)=s} x_r &\le \kappa_s, \quad \forall s, \label{eq:station}\\
F_i + y_i + \sum_j b_{ij}z_j &\ge \underline{q}\,\mathbb{I}(R_i\ge q_{0.75}),
\label{eq:hotspots}
\end{align}
where \(H_R\) is total available ranger-hours, \(M\) the device inventory, \(U\) the drone-sortie inventory, and \(\kappa_s\) the station capacity. Constraint~\eqref{eq:hotspots} enforces minimum recurrent attention in the highest-risk quartile.

\subsubsection{Staffing Floor Derivation}
Let \(\mathcal{H}\) be the hotspot set and \(f_i^\star\) the required revisit frequency in hotspot zones. If each patrol activation consumes \(2h_r\) ranger-hours and usable field time per ranger per planning period is \(h_{\text{shift}}\rho\), then a conservative staffing floor is
\begin{equation}
n_{\min} \ge \frac{\sum_{i\in\mathcal{H}} f_i^\star \bar{h}_i}{h_{\text{shift}}\rho},
\label{eq:rangermin2}
\end{equation}
where \(\bar{h}_i\) is the representative route-hours needed to cover hotspot zone \(i\). Equation~\eqref{eq:rangermin2} closes the loop between the hotspot constraint \eqref{eq:hotspots} and the staffing requirement in Section 6, and it matches the empirical 34-ranger floor in our baseline scenario.

\subsection{Algorithmic Workflow}
To keep the method reproducible and operationally deployable, we solve the model with a rolling-horizon planner that couples optimization and simulation feedback.

\subsection{Recommended Deployment}
Under the baseline scenario, the model allocates 49\% of patrol-hours to the eastern sector, 31\% to the southern-central corridor, and 20\% to the western and northern sectors. It also places 8 of 12 fixed devices in the top-risk quartile and rotates 4 drone sorties across three hotspot sectors.
