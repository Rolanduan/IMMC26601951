\section{Adaptation to Other Protected Areas}
To demonstrate generality, we transfer the framework to Yellowstone National Park in North America and Kakadu National Park in Australia. The architecture remains unchanged:
\subsection{Invariant Model Components}
\begin{itemize}
  \item spatial zoning,
  \item ecological value scoring,
  \item access-based threat priors,
  \item constrained resource allocation,
  \item dynamic simulation and sensitivity analysis.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Modified Inputs, Parameters, and Data Needs}
What changes are the park-specific layers and parameter values:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Yellowstone}: winter access, visitor pressure, road closures, and species-specific conflict patterns alter \(A_i\), \(D_i\), and seasonal multipliers \citep{yellowstoneOfficial}.
  \item \textbf{Kakadu}: wet--dry season flooding, river access, and habitat fragmentation alter the mobility network and seasonal threat timing \citep{kakaduOfficial}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Expected Metric and Deployment Shifts}
Under Yellowstone-like conditions, we expect stronger seasonal oscillation in \(\Resp\) due to winter mobility limits and tourist-driven pressure spikes. Under Kakadu-like wet--dry transitions, deployment should shift toward season-adaptive route families and higher uncertainty buffers in drone allocation. In both transfers, the model predicts that staffing recommendations remain primarily driven by hotspot revisit frequency and response-time feasibility.
