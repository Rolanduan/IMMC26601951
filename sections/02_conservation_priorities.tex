\section{Conservation Priorities}

\subsection{Why Prioritization Matters}

With limited resources, uniform coverage is impossible. We must explicitly answer: \textit{What deserves protection most?} This section establishes priority frameworks for species, locations, and threats.

\subsection{Species Priority Framework}

We prioritize species using IUCN Red List status, ecological role, and flagship value:

\begin{equation}
\pi_s = w_{endangered} \cdot S_{IUCN} + w_{ecological} \cdot S_{keystone} + w_{flagship} \cdot S_{tourism}
\label{eq:species_priority}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Location Priority Framework}

Location priority combines species presence, habitat criticality, and threat exposure:

\begin{equation}
\lambda_i = \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}_i} \pi_s \cdot \alpha_s(i) + \beta \cdot H_{critical}(i) + \gamma \cdot T_{exposure}(i)
\label{eq:location_priority}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\mathcal{S}_i$: Species present at location $i$
\item $\alpha_s(i)$: Abundance/density of species $s$ at location $i$
\item $H_{critical}(i)$: Habitat criticality (breeding grounds, water sources)
\item $T_{exposure}(i)$: Threat exposure (poaching hotspots, access points)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Priority-Driven Resource Allocation Implications}

Prioritization directly informs deployment:
\begin{itemize}
\item High-priority species habitats receive 70\% of patrol resources
\item High-threat locations (boundary entry points, water holes) get continuous monitoring
\item Endangered species (rhinos) trigger rapid-response protocols
\end{itemize}

This is not triage—it is strategic conservation. We protect the most critical assets first, ensuring maximum conservation impact per unit resource invested.
