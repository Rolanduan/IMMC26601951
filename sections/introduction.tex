\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Problem Restatement}

Humanitarian organizations face critical challenges in delivering life-saving supplies (vaccines, medicines, medical equipment) to regions with underdeveloped infrastructure and uncertain road conditions. We address the routing optimization problem for Somalia, where:

\begin{itemize}
\item Road travel times are highly stochastic due to seasonal weather (rainy seasons cause flooding), security incidents (conflict zones), and poor infrastructure (mix of paved and dirt roads)
\item Timely delivery is critical for perishable supplies with limited shelf life
\item Resources are limited: vehicle capacity constraints, fuel costs, and driver availability
\item Reliability is paramount: failed deliveries have life-or-death consequences
\end{itemize}

The problem presents two distinct tasks:

\textbf{Task A (Single Warehouse):} Optimize delivery routes from a central warehouse to multiple distribution points, with vehicles returning to the central warehouse. Minimize expected delivery time while ensuring route reliability $\geq 85\%$.

\textbf{Task B (Multiple Warehouses):} Extend to multiple local warehouses with coordinated delivery strategies. Partition service areas and manage inter-warehouse inventory transfers.

\subsection{Background}

The World Health Organization estimates that over 2 billion people lack access to essential medicines, with inadequate transportation infrastructure being a primary barrier. In Somalia, underdeveloped road infrastructure and political instability create a highly uncertain environment for humanitarian logistics.

Traditional vehicle routing optimization assumes deterministic travel times. However, this approach fails when travel times are stochastic, leading to:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Suboptimal routes:} Shortest paths may fail due to road conditions, requiring emergency re-routing
\item \textbf{Unreliable delivery times:} Communities cannot plan for arrivals, eroding trust
\item \textbf{Increased costs:} Emergency re-routing and failed deliveries increase operational expenses by 15-30\%
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Our Approach}

We develop a \textbf{Stochastic Vehicle Routing Problem (SVRP)} framework with reliability constraints:

\textbf{Progressive Modeling:} We present three models of increasing sophistication:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Model I:} Deterministic VRP baseline (using expected travel times)
\item \textbf{Model II:} Chance-constrained SVRP with reliability threshold $\alpha = 0.85$
\item \textbf{Model III:} Two-stage stochastic program with recourse decisions
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Data-Driven:} We fit lognormal distributions to 5 years of Somalia road network data (147 segments, 1,826 daily observations per segment). Table 1 summarizes the data characteristics.

\textbf{Algorithm:} We employ Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) metaheuristic with scenario-based sampling (100 scenarios for optimization, 1,000 for validation). This achieves near-optimal solutions (optimality gap $< 4\%$) within 3-4 minutes for instances up to 60 customers.

\subsection{Objectives}

Our primary objectives are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Minimize expected total delivery time across all routes
\item Ensure route reliability $\geq 85\%$ (probability of on-time completion)
\item Compare single-warehouse vs. multi-warehouse strategies
\item Identify efficiency-reliability-cost trade-offs through multi-objective optimization
\end{enumerate}
