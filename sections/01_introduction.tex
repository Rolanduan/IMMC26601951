\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Problem Context}

Large national parks face an existential challenge: protecting wildlife across vast, geographically complex terrain with limited resources. \textbf{Etosha National Park} in Namibia—22,270 km$^2$ of savanna, salt pans, and diverse ecosystems—exemplifies this challenge. The park hosts critically endangered black rhinos, elephants, lions, and other species facing persistent poaching threats.

Traditional uniform patrol coverage fails due to:
\begin{itemize}
\item Resource constraints preventing complete coverage
\item Geographic barriers (limited roads, seasonal water scarcity)
\item Stochastic threat patterns requiring adaptive responses
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Problem Statement}

\textit{How should we strategically deploy conservation assets to achieve quantifiable protection under uncertainty?}

\noindent We address:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Define "protection" in measurable terms
\item Develop strategic resource allocation models
\item Evaluate protection over time and infer staffing needs
\item Test robustness under uncertainty
\item Demonstrate transferability to other reserves
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Our Approach}

We propose a \textbf{priority-based strategic allocation framework} integrating:
\begin{itemize}
\item Multi-dimensional protection metrics (spatial, temporal, threat-specific)
\item Genetic algorithm optimization for deployment
\item Dynamic staffing inference from target protection levels
\item Comprehensive robustness testing
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Key Results}

Strategic allocation achieves:
\begin{itemize}
\item 89\% high-priority species protection (vs. 62\% uniform)
\item 94\% critical area coverage (vs. 58\% uniform)
\item 81\% threat detection rate (vs. 55\% uniform)
\item 320 km$^2$/ranger efficiency (vs. 180\% uniform)
\item Minimum staffing: 127 rangers
\end{itemize}
